How to invest during the coast phase
Once you hit Coast FIRE, you no longer need to save — but how you handle your existing portfolio still matters enormously.
The Coast FIRE portfolio question: Should you shift to more conservative investments once you stop contributing? Most experts say no — you still have decades of compounding ahead. A 30-year-old who hits Coast FIRE at $150K should stay mostly in equities for another 20-30 years.
1
Ages 20-45: 90-100% equities
Total market index funds (VTI, VXUS). You have decades to recover from downturns. Bonds reduce your expected return without meaningful protection at this age.
2
Ages 45-55: 80% equities, 20% bonds
Begin adding short-duration bond funds. This reduces sequence-of-returns risk as you approach retirement without sacrificing too much growth.
3
Ages 55-65+: Build the bond tent
Increase bonds to 30-40% in the 5 years before retirement (the "bond tent"), then gradually reduce back to 60-70% equities post-retirement. This protects against a crash in your first retirement years.
1
Keep maxing Roth IRA ($7,000/yr)
Even after Coast FIRE, if you're still working part-time, max your Roth IRA. Tax-free growth for decades is extremely valuable. Roth withdrawals don't affect your ACA subsidy calculation either.
2
Consider Roth conversions
During low-income coast years, convert Traditional IRA/401k money to Roth. You're in a lower tax bracket now, and the money grows tax-free afterward. The ladder takes 5 years to season.
3
Taxable brokerage is your bridge
If you need income before 59.5, access taxable brokerage gains (LTCG at 0-15%) before touching retirement accounts. FIFO cost basis often means much of early withdrawals is return of principal.
Rebalance annually, not reactively. Set a calendar reminder for January 1st. Sell what's overweight, buy what's underweight. Never rebalance in response to market news — that's just emotional trading with extra steps.
Tax-efficient rebalancing: Rebalance inside tax-advantaged accounts first (no tax consequences). In taxable accounts, direct new dividends to underweight assets instead of selling.
Bands, not calendar: Some prefer 5% threshold bands — rebalance only when an asset class drifts more than 5% from target. This reduces unnecessary transactions.
Tax loss harvesting: When markets drop, harvest losses in taxable accounts. Sell a losing position, buy a similar (not identical) fund, and book the tax loss while maintaining market exposure.
Mistake: Shifting to cash or bonds too early
A 35-year-old with $200K who moves to 50% bonds loses approximately $1.2M in expected terminal wealth by age 65, assuming 7% equity vs 3.5% bond returns. You need decades of equity exposure.
Mistake: Stopping all contributions in high-income years
Coast FIRE means you CAN stop — not that you MUST. If you're earning well and enjoying your work, keep contributing. Every extra dollar invested during high-earning years compounds to significant wealth.
Mistake: Using nominal returns instead of real returns
If you use 10% (nominal S&P 500 return) to calculate your coast number, you'll undershoot. Use 7% real (inflation-adjusted). Your spending needs at 65 will be higher than today due to inflation — the portfolio needs to keep up.
Everything you need to know about Coast FIRE
The complete guide — from the math to the psychology to the practical execution.

What is Coast FIRE?

Coast FIRE (Financial Independence, Retire Early) is the point at which you have accumulated enough in investment accounts that you no longer need to contribute anything more. Your portfolio will grow on its own — through compound interest — to reach your full FIRE number by your target retirement age.

How is the Coast FIRE number calculated?

Coast FIRE Number = FIRE Target / (1 + Real Return Rate) ^ Years to Retirement. For example: if you need $1.5M by age 65 and you're 35 (30 years), at 7% real return: $1,500,000 / (1.07)^30 = approximately $197,000. Invest $197,000 today, contribute nothing else, and your portfolio reaches $1.5M by 65.

Coast FIRE vs Lean FIRE vs Regular FIRE

Coast FIRE is a milestone on the journey — not a final destination. It's unique because it doesn't require you to fully retire immediately. After hitting your Coast number, you still work — but only enough to cover current living expenses, with zero pressure to save. This is why Coast FIRE is especially popular with people who want to leave high-stress careers, take sabbaticals, or transition to passion-based work without financial anxiety.
