What the Covered Call Calculator Does

Covered calls are simultaneously one of the most popular and most commonly miscalculated options strategies in retail trading. Traders frequently know the premium they're collecting but don't work through the full math: the effective cost basis after premium, the exact breakeven on the position, the maximum profit scenario, and most importantly, the annualized return — which is the only apples-to-apples way to compare one covered call opportunity to another.

The Covered Call Calculator computes all of this in real time from four inputs: stock purchase price, strike price, premium received, and days until expiration. Every output updates instantly as you adjust any input.

The breakeven price ($137.15 in the example above) is the stock price at which you neither gain nor lose on the combined position — stock plus option — at expiration. It's the number that defines your real downside protection from the premium collected. Many traders think of the strike price as their "safety zone" but the breakeven is the more important number for risk management.

The annualized return converts the premium yield to a per-year figure, accounting for the number of days until expiration. This makes it possible to compare a 21-day 2% yield to a 45-day 3.5% yield on an equal footing. Without annualizing, every comparison is apples to oranges.

Built for the Wheel Strategy

The covered call calculator is intentionally designed around the wheel strategy — the approach of selling cash-secured puts on a stock you want to own, then selling covered calls if assigned, repeating the cycle to collect premium indefinitely. This strategy is popular with retail investors because it generates income on positions you'd hold anyway while providing a modest downside buffer through premium collection.

For wheel traders, the most important calculation is the effective purchase price — the break-even cost basis after accounting for all premium collected across multiple put and call cycles. The calculator's effective cost basis output tracks this directly, making it straightforward to see whether your wheel position is net-profitable across multiple rolls.

"Covered calls don't protect you from a real drawdown. They protect you from time decay and small moves. Know the difference before you sell."
