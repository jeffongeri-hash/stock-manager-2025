Two of the biggest financial decisions most people make outside of retirement savings are buying a rental property and financing a vehicle. Both are routinely under-analyzed. Why Most Rental Property Investors Get the Math Wrong

The most common mistake in rental property analysis is confusing cash flow with profit. A property that rents for $2,200 per month with a $1,800 mortgage payment looks cash-flow positive on a napkin. But subtract property taxes, insurance, maintenance reserves, vacancy allowance, and property management fees, and many "positive cash flow" properties are actually negative — or so marginally positive that the capital would generate better returns in an index fund with no management overhead.

The cap rate (net operating income divided by purchase price) is the most standardized comparison metric in real estate — it measures what the property returns independent of financing. In most markets, a cap rate below 5% indicates a speculative appreciation play rather than an income investment. Above 7–8% is generally strong for residential real estate.

The cash-on-cash return measures what your actual deployed cash earns annually, accounting for leverage. A 20% down payment on a leveraged property can generate a cash-on-cash return of 8–12% even when the cap rate is 5–6%, which is why real estate investing at reasonable leverage often outperforms equivalent unlevered returns. But this metric deteriorates quickly as vacancy rates rise or interest rates increase — which is why the calculator models both.

The Break-Even Occupancy Rate

One output that novice real estate investors frequently overlook is the break-even occupancy rate — the minimum percentage of time the property must be rented for the investment to remain cash-flow neutral. A property with a 94% break-even occupancy rate has almost no margin for vacancies; one with an 82% break-even rate can sustain extended vacancies and still service the debt. This number is the most honest measure of how fragile or resilient any given rental investment is.

"The cap rate tells you what a property is worth. The break-even occupancy rate tells you how much adversity it can handle before it costs you money."

FIRE Integration: Real Estate in Your Retirement Plan

For FIRE planners, rental income is one of the most valuable assets in a retirement plan because it's inflation-hedged, partially tax-advantaged through depreciation, and not subject to sequence-of-returns risk the way equity portfolios are. The real estate calculator integrates with the FIRE Planning Suite — rental cash flow can be added as a passive income stream that reduces your required portfolio size, directly lowering your FIRE number.

The Car Finance Calculator: The True Cost of Your Next Vehicle

The automobile is the single largest wealth-destroying purchase most households make. Not because cars are inherently bad purchases, but because financing structures are deliberately designed to obscure the true cost — and most buyers make decisions based on monthly payment rather than total cost of ownership.

A $450 monthly payment sounds manageable. Over 72 months at 7.9% interest, that same payment represents $32,400 in total payments on a $28,000 vehicle — $4,400 in pure interest — on a depreciating asset that will be worth 45% less by the time the loan is paid off.
