Strike Selection Guide
The strike you choose determines everything — premium collected, probability of assignment, and whether you're optimizing for income or capital gains.
In-the-Money (ITM) Strikes — Below current stock price
Higher premium. Higher probability of assignment. Provides more downside protection. Best for: bearish-neutral outlook, maximum income, volatile markets where you want more cushion.
At-the-Money (ATM) Strikes — Near current stock price
Highest premium as a percentage of stock price. High probability of assignment (~50%). Best for: neutral outlook, maximum immediate income regardless of direction.
Out-of-the-Money (OTM) Strikes — Above current stock price
Lower premium but participates in upside. Lower assignment probability. Best for: bullish outlook, want to keep shares but collect income, classic income generation strategy.
Rolling Strategies
When your covered call is at risk of assignment — or you want to extend your income — rolling lets you close the current position and open a new one to collect more premium.

The 21-day rule: Many experienced options traders roll covered calls when they reach 21 DTE, regardless of profit level. At 21 DTE, theta decay accelerates significantly — closing and re-opening at 30-45 DTE captures maximum time value on the new position while avoiding gamma risk near expiration.
Earnings Avoidance Calendar
Selling covered calls through earnings is extremely risky — implied volatility is artificially elevated (IV crush risk) and the stock can move 10-20% in either direction overnight. Always check earnings dates before selling.
IV Crush warning: After earnings are announced, implied volatility typically collapses 30-60% regardless of whether the move was expected. If you sold a covered call with inflated IV and the stock stays flat through earnings, your option loses value rapidly — which is actually good if you're the seller. But if you're LONG options through earnings, IV crush destroys your position.
Popular Covered Call Stocks — Earnings Dates
Safe rule: Only sell covered calls that expire BEFORE the earnings date, or AFTER earnings (to capture IV crush as premium seller). Never straddle an earnings announcement with a short call position.

Tax Considerations
Covered call tax treatment is more complex than it appears. The qualified covered call rules affect your holding period and can convert long-term gains to short-term.
Premium Income is Short-Term
All covered call premium is taxed as short-term capital gains, regardless of how long you've held the stock. This is ordinary income tax rates for most taxpayers.
Qualified Covered Call Rules (IRC Section 1092)
If you sell a call that is "not qualified" (too deep ITM), it suspends your holding period on the underlying shares. This can convert a long-term capital gain on the stock to short-term when/if called away. Qualified calls must have a strike above a specific level relative to the stock price and must have more than 30 days to expiration.
Best Practice for Tax Efficiency
Sell OTM covered calls (5-10% above stock price) with 30+ DTE. This satisfies qualified covered call rules and preserves your long-term capital gains treatment on the underlying. Use tax-advantaged accounts (IRA) for premium income to avoid short-term tax drag.
Wash Sale Rules Don't Apply to Options Premiums
The wash sale rule (which prevents claiming losses if you rebuy within 30 days) does not apply to covered call premiums. However, if you sell a covered call on a stock you've taken a loss on and the call creates a "substantially identical" security situation, wash sale rules may apply to the underlying stock loss.
Learn Covered Calls

How does a covered call work?

A covered call is an options strategy where you own at least 100 shares of a stock and sell (write) one call option contract for every 100 shares. You receive the premium from the buyer immediately. In exchange, you agree to sell your shares at the strike price if the stock rises above it by expiration. If it doesn't, the option expires worthless and you keep both your shares and the premium.

What is the maximum profit on a covered call?

Maximum profit = (Strike Price - Cost Basis) × 100 + Premium Received. For example: own 100 shares at $175, sell the $200 call for $2.85 premium. If called away: ($200 - $175) × 100 + $285 = $2,785 maximum profit.

What is the breakeven on a covered call?

Breakeven = Cost Basis - Premium Received. If you paid $175/share and received $2.85 in premium, your breakeven is $172.15. This means even if the stock drops to $172.15, you neither profit nor lose from the entire covered call position.
